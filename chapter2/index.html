2. The War of the Romantics#

Quick Read

Time: 10 min

The War of the Romantics (German: Parteienstreit) was a 19th-century debate about the future of music after Beethoven. It divided composers, critics, and audiences into opposing camps-and generated the correspondence networks preserved in the SoNAR database.

The Two Camps#

“Absolute Music” (Brahms)

“Music of the Future” (Wagner)

Music as pure form

Music as expression

Classical traditions

Innovation and progress

Symphony, sonata, chamber music

Opera, tone poem, music drama

Allies: Joseph Joachim, Clara Schumann, Eduard Hanslick

Allies: Franz Liszt, Anton Bruckner, Hugo Wolf

In 1860, Brahms and Joachim published a manifesto rejecting the “New German School”-marking the open break between camps.

Timeline#

1850s

Franz Liszt establishes Weimar as progressive center; Richard Wagner develops theories

1860

Johannes Brahms and Joseph Joachim publish manifesto against the “New German School”

1870s

Brahms rises with symphonies; conflict intensifies

1883

Wagner dies; conflict begins to soften

1890s

Next generation synthesizes both traditions

Why Correspondence Matters#

Before telephones and email, letters were how musicians stayed connected-for professional business, artistic exchange, alliance building, and personal friendship.

Interpretation Caution

  • No edge does not mean no relationship-letters may be lost

  • Famous composers’ letters survived more often-survival bias

  • Edges don’t show relationship quality-friendly or hostile look the same

Summary#

Key Points

  • Two camps: “Absolute Music” (Brahms) vs. “Music of the Future” (Wagner)

  • 1860 manifesto marked the open break

  • Key figures: Johannes Brahms, Richard Wagner, Franz Liszt, Clara Schumann, Joseph Joachim

  • Correspondence reveals networks-but with survival bias
